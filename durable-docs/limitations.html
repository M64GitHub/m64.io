Limitations & how we compare

@synadia-ai/durable is a deliberately small correctness core, not a feature-complete platform. Here's a straight accounting of what it doesn't do — measured against Temporal (the durable-execution benchmark) and Inngest (an event-driven durable-workflow platform) — and what you get in exchange.

#The trade-off, up front

Most of what's missing is the price of the core bet: there is no durable-execution server and no database to operate — just the NATS you already run. The whole engine is a library over one NATS primitive (an optimistic append), with a frozen, language-neutral wire format. That buys radical operational simplicity and an auditable correctness model; it costs the deep feature surface that a dedicated control plane like Temporal or a hosted platform like Inngest can offer. This page is about that gap.

#The gaps that matter most

Three of these aren't ergonomic nice-to-haves — they're load-bearing for real production systems:

  • No versioning of live runs. Every entry carries a wfVersion and replay asserts a stable version, but there's no getVersion/patched equivalent — so you can't safely change a workflow that has in-flight runs. Both competitors solve this; without it, deploys to long-running workflows are risky.
  • No flow control. Durable fan-out now exists (ctx.startCall/startSleep/startAwait + native Promise.all / ctx.race), but there is still no concurrency limit, rate limit, throttle, debounce, or batching anywhere. Inngest's flow control is its headline. Global limits are genuinely harder in a library-on-NATS model with no central coordinator.
  • Unproven in production. It's unpublished — there's no managed / hosted option and no track record at scale yet, and you operate (and must size / replicate) the NATS it rides on. The correctness core is heavily tested (202 tests), but “tested” isn't “battle-tested.”

#Limitations in detail

#Execution model

  • Fan-out has edges. ctx.startCall/startSleep/startAwait + native Promise.all/Promise.allSettled and ctx.race fan out durably — but race losers run to completion (no ctx.race({ cancelLosers }) yet), and local-step (ctx.run) fan-out (ctx.startRun) isn't built (wrap parallel local work in one ctx.run(() => Promise.all([...]))).
  • Cancellation cleanup is local-only. client.cancel hard-stops a run and cascades to in-flight children, and ctx.onCancel runs LIFO compensation — but handlers are limited to local ctx.run steps today; a suspending compensation (calling a child or sleeping inside a handler) is deferred.
  • No long-running-activity support — AckWait defaults to 30 s and there's no working()/heartbeat to extend it. A step that overruns AckWait is redelivered and re-executed (safe via ctx.key(), but wasteful).
  • Thin retry/error model — none | fixed | exponential; no non-retryable-error classes, retry budgets, or onFailure / dead-letter hook.
  • Children must be pre-registered on a worker and have no detached / abandon policy — though ctx.startCall runs them concurrently and client.cancel now cascades to in-flight children.

#Triggering, observability & control

  • Imperative, not event-driven — runs begin with client.start(); there's no declarative event trigger or one-event-to-many fan-out, and ctx.await matches by name (FIFO), not by a predicate over event payloads.
  • Observability is status() + the journal (plus the dashboard) — no arbitrary queries into running state, no synchronous Update, and no search/visibility queries across runs.
  • Scheduling is DIY — cron-style cadence is built from ctx.sleep + continueAsNew, not a first-class Schedules feature.

#Scale & operations (sharp edges)

  • Wider at-least-once window. No gating KV lease (DESIGN Q6, deferred) — correctness holds via the journal CAS, but under concurrent multi-worker load a step can re-execute, so effects lean on ctx.key() idempotency more than they would with a lease.
  • No GC of finished generations. continueAsNew bounds each generation's journal, but old generations' logs linger — a slow storage leak for long-lived chains.
  • 8 MiB payload ceiling, no object-store offload yet.
  • TypeScript only — the wire spec is language-neutral, but no Go/Python SDK ships today.
  • Unpublished — no managed option and no production track record yet.

#Roadmap vs. fundamental

It helps to separate “just not built” from “harder by design”:

  • On the roadmap (hooks left). The gating lease, in-flight versioning, generation GC, object-store offload, suspending ctx.onCancel cleanup, and ctx.startRun local-step fan-out all have designs and are explicit non-goals for now — not architectural dead-ends.
  • Harder given the model. Global flow control (cluster-wide concurrency / rate limits) and rich cross-run visibility/search lean on a central control plane; doing them well over a library-on-NATS substrate is real work, not a flag flip.
  • Deliberate, not a gap. “No server, no database, TS-first, you operate NATS” is the design, not an oversight — it's the reason the rest is small.

#When durable is the right call — and when it isn't

  • Reach for it when you want crash-safe, exactly-once multi-step workflows without standing up and operating a new control plane + database — and you already run NATS. The correctness core (steps, timers, signals, child workflows, durable fan-out, cancellation, continue-as-new, crash-replay) is solid and tested.
  • Prefer Temporal when you need versioning of live workflows, fine-grained cancellation scopes, queries/Update, mature multi-language SDKs, and battle-tested scale — and a dedicated server + DB is acceptable.
  • Prefer Inngest when your work is event-driven and you need flow control (concurrency, throttling, debounce, batching) and a serverless/hosted execution model out of the box.

The honest short version: durable trades breadth for operational simplicity and a small, correct, portable core. See the Temporal mapping, how state is persisted, and the design doc (§9, non-goals) for the full picture.
